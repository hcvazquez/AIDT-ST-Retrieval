         <h1 class="entry-title">Machine Learning :: Cosine Similarity for Vector Space Models (Part III)</h1>
         <div class="entry-meta">
           Posted 
          <span class="posted-on">on <a href="http://blog.christianperone.com/2013/09/machine-learning-cosine-similarity-for-vector-space-models-part-iii/" rel="bookmark"><time class="entry-date published updated" datetime="2013-09-12T14:36:16+00:00">12/09/2013</time></a> </span> 
          <span class="byline">by <span class="author vcard"> <a class="url fn n" href="http://blog.christianperone.com/author/admin/" title="View all posts by Christian S. Perone"><span class="author-name">Christian S. Perone</span></a> </span> </span> 
          <span class="entry-meta-sep"> / </span> 
          <span class="comments-link"> <a href="http://blog.christianperone.com/2013/09/machine-learning-cosine-similarity-for-vector-space-models-part-iii/#comments">50 Comments</a> </span>
         </div>
        </header>
        <div class="entry-content">
         <p>* It has been a long time since I wrote the TF-IDF tutorial (<a title="Machine Learning :: Text feature extraction (tf-idf)  Part I" href="http://blog.christianperone.com/?p=1589">Part I</a> and <a title="Machine Learning :: Text feature extraction (tf-idf)  Part II" href="http://blog.christianperone.com/?p=1747">Part II</a>) and as I promissed, here is the continuation of the tutorial. Unfortunately I had no time to fix the previous tutorials for the newer versions of the <a title="scikit-learn" href="http://scikit-learn.org/stable/" target="_blank">scikit-learn (sklearn)</a> package nor to answer all the questions, but I hope to do that in a close future.</p>
         <p>So, on the previous tutorials we learned how a document can be modeled in the Vector Space, how the TF-IDF transformation works and how the TF-IDF is calculated, now what we are going to learn is how to use a well-known similarity measure (Cosine Similarity) to calculate the similarity between different documents.</p>
         <h2>The Dot Product</h2>
         <p>Lets begin with the definition of the dot product for two vectors: <img src="http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cvec%7Ba%7D+%3D+%28a_1%2C+a_2%2C+a_3%2C+%5Cldots%29&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=000000&amp;s=0" alt="\vec{a} = (a_1, a_2, a_3, \ldots)" title="\vec{a} = (a_1, a_2, a_3, \ldots)" class="latex"> and&nbsp;<img src="http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cvec%7Bb%7D+%3D+%28b_1%2C+b_2%2C+b_3%2C+%5Cldots%29&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=000000&amp;s=0" alt="\vec{b} = (b_1, b_2, b_3, \ldots)" title="\vec{b} = (b_1, b_2, b_3, \ldots)" class="latex">, where <img src="http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=a_n&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=000000&amp;s=0" alt="a_n" title="a_n" class="latex"> and <img src="http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=b_n&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=000000&amp;s=0" alt="b_n" title="b_n" class="latex"> are the components of the vector (features of the document, or TF-IDF values for each word of the document in our example) and the <img src="http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cmathit%7Bn%7D&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=000000&amp;s=0" alt="\mathit{n}" title="\mathit{n}" class="latex"> is the dimension of the vectors:</p>
         <p style="text-align: center;"><img src="http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=++%5Cvec%7Ba%7D+%5Ccdot+%5Cvec%7Bb%7D+%3D+%5Csum_%7Bi%3D1%7D%5En+a_ib_i+%3D+a_1b_1+%2B+a_2b_2+%2B+%5Ccdots+%2B+a_nb_n++&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=000000&amp;s=1" alt="  \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = \sum_{i=1}^n a_ib_i = a_1b_1 + a_2b_2 + \cdots + a_nb_n  " title="  \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = \sum_{i=1}^n a_ib_i = a_1b_1 + a_2b_2 + \cdots + a_nb_n  " class="latex"></p>
         <p>As you can see, the definition of the dot product is a simple multiplication of each component from the both vectors added together. See an example of a dot product for two vectors with 2 dimensions each (2D):</p>
         <p style="text-align: center;"><img src="http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=++%5Cvec%7Ba%7D+%3D+%280%2C+3%29+%5C%5C+++%5Cvec%7Bb%7D+%3D+%284%2C+0%29+%5C%5C+++%5Cvec%7Ba%7D+%5Ccdot+%5Cvec%7Bb%7D+%3D+0%2A4+%2B+3%2A0+%3D+0++&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=000000&amp;s=0" alt="  \vec{a} = (0, 3) \\   \vec{b} = (4, 0) \\   \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = 0*4 + 3*0 = 0  " title="  \vec{a} = (0, 3) \\   \vec{b} = (4, 0) \\   \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = 0*4 + 3*0 = 0  " class="latex"></p>
         <p>The first thing you probably noticed is that the result of a dot product between two vectors isnt another vector but a single value, a scalar.</p>
         <p>This is all very simple and easy to understand, but what is a dot product ? What is the intuitive idea behind it ? What does it mean to have a dot product of zero ? To understand it, we need to understand what is the geometric definition of the dot product:</p>
         <p style="text-align: center;"><img src="http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=++%5Cvec%7Ba%7D+%5Ccdot+%5Cvec%7Bb%7D+%3D+%5C%7C%5Cvec%7Ba%7D%5C%7C%5C%7C%5Cvec%7Bb%7D%5C%7C%5Ccos%7B%5Ctheta%7D++&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=000000&amp;s=0" alt="  \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = \|\vec{a}\|\|\vec{b}\|\cos{\theta}  " title="  \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = \|\vec{a}\|\|\vec{b}\|\cos{\theta}  " class="latex"></p>
         <p>Rearranging the equation to understand it better using the commutative property, we have:</p>
         <p style="text-align: center;"><img src="http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=++%5Cvec%7Ba%7D+%5Ccdot+%5Cvec%7Bb%7D+%3D+%5C%7C%5Cvec%7Bb%7D%5C%7C%5C%7C%5Cvec%7Ba%7D%5C%7C%5Ccos%7B%5Ctheta%7D++&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=000000&amp;s=0" alt="  \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = \|\vec{b}\|\|\vec{a}\|\cos{\theta}  " title="  \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = \|\vec{b}\|\|\vec{a}\|\cos{\theta}  " class="latex"></p>
         <p>So, what is the term <img src="http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cdisplaystyle+%5C%7C%5Cvec%7Ba%7D%5C%7C%5Ccos%7B%5Ctheta%7D&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=000000&amp;s=0" alt="\displaystyle \|\vec{a}\|\cos{\theta}" title="\displaystyle \|\vec{a}\|\cos{\theta}" class="latex"> ? This term is the projection of the vector <img src="http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cvec%7Ba%7D&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=000000&amp;s=0" alt="\vec{a}" title="\vec{a}" class="latex"> into the vector <img src="http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cvec%7Bb%7D&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=000000&amp;s=0" alt="\vec{b}" title="\vec{b}" class="latex"> as shown on the image below:</p>
         <figure id="attachment_2560" style="width: 300px" class="wp-caption aligncenter">
          <a href="http://blog.christianperone.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/09/Dot_Product.png"><img class="size-full wp-image-2560" title="Dot_Product" src="http://blog.christianperone.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/09/Dot_Product.png" alt="" width="300" height="240"></a>
          <figcaption class="wp-caption-text">
           The projection of the vector A into the vector B. By Wikipedia.
          </figcaption>
         </figure>
         <p>Now, what happens when the vector <img src="http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cvec%7Ba%7D&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=000000&amp;s=0" alt="\vec{a}" title="\vec{a}" class="latex"> is orthogonal (with an angle of 90 degrees) to the vector <img src="http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cvec%7Bb%7D&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=000000&amp;s=0" alt="\vec{b}" title="\vec{b}" class="latex"> like on the image below ?</p>
         <figure id="attachment_2580" style="width: 239px" class="wp-caption aligncenter">
          <a href="http://blog.christianperone.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/09/vectors1.gif"><img class="size-full wp-image-2580" title="vectors1" src="http://blog.christianperone.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/09/vectors1.gif" alt="" width="239" height="238"></a>
          <figcaption class="wp-caption-text">
           Two orthogonal vectors (with 90 degrees angle).
          </figcaption>
         </figure>
         <p>There will be no adjacent side on the triangle, it will be equivalent to zero, the term <img src="http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cdisplaystyle+%5C%7C%5Cvec%7Ba%7D%5C%7C%5Ccos%7B%5Ctheta%7D&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=000000&amp;s=0" alt="\displaystyle \|\vec{a}\|\cos{\theta}" title="\displaystyle \|\vec{a}\|\cos{\theta}" class="latex"> will be zero and the resulting multiplication with the magnitude of the vector <img src="http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Cvec%7Bb%7D&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=000000&amp;s=0" alt="\vec{b}" title="\vec{b}" class="latex"> will also be zero. Now you know that, when the dot product between two different vectors is zero, they are orthogonal to each other (they have an angle of 90 degrees), this is a very neat way to check the orthogonality of different vectors. It is also important to note that we are using 2D examples, but the most amazing fact about it is that we can also calculate angles and similarity between vectors in higher dimensional spaces, and that is why math let us see far than the obvious even when we cant visualize or imagine what is the angle between two vectors with twelve dimensions for instance.</p>
         <h2>The Cosine Similarity</h2>
         <p>The cosine similarity between two vectors (or two documents on the Vector Space) is a measure that calculates the cosine of the angle between them. This metric is a measurement of orientation and not magnitude, it can be seen as a comparison between documents on a normalized space because were not taking into the consideration only the magnitude of each word count (tf-idf) of each document, but the angle between the documents. What we have to do to build the cosine similarity equation is to solve the equation of the dot product for the <img src="http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Ccos%7B%5Ctheta%7D&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=000000&amp;s=0" alt="\cos{\theta}" title="\cos{\theta}" class="latex">:</p>
         <p style="text-align: center;"><img src="http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=++%5Cdisplaystyle++%5Cvec%7Ba%7D+%5Ccdot+%5Cvec%7Bb%7D+%3D+%5C%7C%5Cvec%7Ba%7D%5C%7C%5C%7C%5Cvec%7Bb%7D%5C%7C%5Ccos%7B%5Ctheta%7D+%5C%5C+%5C%5C++%5Ccos%7B%5Ctheta%7D+%3D+%5Cfrac%7B%5Cvec%7Ba%7D+%5Ccdot+%5Cvec%7Bb%7D%7D%7B%5C%7C%5Cvec%7Ba%7D%5C%7C%5C%7C%5Cvec%7Bb%7D%5C%7C%7D++&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=000000&amp;s=0" alt="  \displaystyle  \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = \|\vec{a}\|\|\vec{b}\|\cos{\theta} \\ \\  \cos{\theta} = \frac{\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b}}{\|\vec{a}\|\|\vec{b}\|}  " title="  \displaystyle  \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = \|\vec{a}\|\|\vec{b}\|\cos{\theta} \\ \\  \cos{\theta} = \frac{\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b}}{\|\vec{a}\|\|\vec{b}\|}  " class="latex"></p>
         <p>And that is it, this is the cosine similarity formula. Cosine Similarity will generate a metric that says how related are two documents by looking at the angle instead of magnitude, like in the examples below:</p>
         <figure id="attachment_2599" style="width: 890px" class="wp-caption aligncenter">
          <a href="http://blog.christianperone.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/09/cosinesimilarityfq1.png"><img class="size-full wp-image-2599" title="cosinesimilarityfq1" src="http://blog.christianperone.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/09/cosinesimilarityfq1.png" alt="" width="890" height="250"></a>
          <figcaption class="wp-caption-text">
           The Cosine Similarity values for different documents, 1 (same direction), 0 (90 deg.), -1 (opposite directions).
          </figcaption>
         </figure>
         <p>Note that even if we had a vector pointing to a point far from another vector, they still could have an small angle and that is the central point on the use of Cosine Similarity, the measurement tends to ignore the higher term count on documents. Suppose we have a document with the word sky appearing 200 times and another document with the word sky appearing 50, the Euclidean distance between them will be higher but the angle will still be small because they are pointing to the same direction, which is what matters when we are comparing documents.</p>
         <p>Now that we have a Vector Space Model of documents (like on the image below) modeled as vectors (with TF-IDF counts) and also have a formula to calculate the similarity between different documents in this space, lets see now how we do it in practice using <a title="Scikit-Learn" href="http://scikit-learn.org" target="_blank">scikit-learn (sklearn)</a>.</p>
         <figure id="attachment_2582" style="width: 504px" class="wp-caption aligncenter">
          <a href="http://blog.christianperone.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/09/vector_space.png"><img class=" wp-image-2582 " title="vector_space" src="http://blog.christianperone.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/09/vector_space.png" alt="" width="504" height="378"></a>
          <figcaption class="wp-caption-text">
           Vector Space Model
          </figcaption>
         </figure>
         <h2>Practice Using Scikit-learn (sklearn)</h2>
         <p>* In this tutorial Im using the Python 2.7.5 and Scikit-learn 0.14.1.</p>
         <p>The first thing we need to do is to define our set of example documents:</p>
         <pre class="EnlighterJSRAW" data-enlighter-language="python">documents = (
"The sky is blue",
"The sun is bright",
"The sun in the sky is bright",
"We can see the shining sun, the bright sun"
)</pre>
         <p>And then we instantiate the Sklearn TF-IDF Vectorizer and transform our documents into the TF-IDF matrix:</p>
         <pre class="EnlighterJSRAW" data-enlighter-language="python">from sklearn.feature_extraction.text import TfidfVectorizer
tfidf_vectorizer = TfidfVectorizer()
tfidf_matrix = tfidf_vectorizer.fit_transform(documents)
print tfidf_matrix.shape
(4, 11)</pre>
         <p>Now we have the TF-IDF matrix (<strong>tfidf_matrix</strong>) for each document (the number of rows of the matrix) with 11 tf-idf terms (the number of columns from the matrix), we can calculate the Cosine Similarity between the first document (The sky is blue) with each of the other documents of the set:</p>
         <pre class="EnlighterJSRAW" data-enlighter-language="python">from sklearn.metrics.pairwise import cosine_similarity
cosine_similarity(tfidf_matrix[0:1], tfidf_matrix)
array([[ 1. &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp;, &nbsp;0.36651513, &nbsp;0.52305744, &nbsp;0.13448867]])</pre>
         <p>The <strong>tfidf_matrix[0:1]&nbsp;</strong>is the Scipy operation to get the first row of the sparse matrix and the resulting array is the Cosine Similarity between the first document with all documents in the set. Note that the first value of the array is 1.0 because it is the Cosine Similarity between the first document with itself. Also note that due to the presence of similar words on the third document (The sun in the sky is bright), it achieved a better score.</p>
         <p>If you want, you can also solve the Cosine Similarity for the angle between vectors:</p>
         <p style="text-align: center;"><img src="http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=++%5Ccos%7B%5Ctheta%7D+%3D+%5Cfrac%7B%5Cvec%7Ba%7D+%5Ccdot+%5Cvec%7Bb%7D%7D%7B%5C%7C%5Cvec%7Ba%7D%5C%7C%5C%7C%5Cvec%7Bb%7D%5C%7C%7D++&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=000000&amp;s=1" alt="  \cos{\theta} = \frac{\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b}}{\|\vec{a}\|\|\vec{b}\|}  " title="  \cos{\theta} = \frac{\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b}}{\|\vec{a}\|\|\vec{b}\|}  " class="latex"></p>
         <p>We only need to isolate the angle (<img src="http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Ctheta&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=000000&amp;s=0" alt="\theta" title="\theta" class="latex">) and move the <img src="http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Ccos&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=000000&amp;s=0" alt="\cos" title="\cos" class="latex"> to the right hand of the equation:</p>
         <p style="text-align: center;"><img src="http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=++%5Ctheta+%3D+%5Carccos%7B%5Cfrac%7B%5Cvec%7Ba%7D+%5Ccdot+%5Cvec%7Bb%7D%7D%7B%5C%7C%5Cvec%7Ba%7D%5C%7C%5C%7C%5Cvec%7Bb%7D%5C%7C%7D%7D++&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=000000&amp;s=1" alt="  \theta = \arccos{\frac{\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b}}{\|\vec{a}\|\|\vec{b}\|}}  " title="  \theta = \arccos{\frac{\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b}}{\|\vec{a}\|\|\vec{b}\|}}  " class="latex"></p>
         <p>The <img src="http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Carccos&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=000000&amp;s=0" alt="\arccos" title="\arccos" class="latex"> is the same as the inverse of the cosine (<img src="http://s0.wp.com/latex.php?latex=%5Ccos%5E-1&amp;bg=ffffff&amp;fg=000000&amp;s=0" alt="\cos^-1" title="\cos^-1" class="latex">).</p>
         <div>
          &nbsp;Lets for instance, check the angle between the first and third documents:
         </div>
         <div></div>
         <div>
          <pre class="EnlighterJSRAW" data-enlighter-language="python">import math
# This was already calculated on the previous step, so we just use the value
cos_sim = 0.52305744
angle_in_radians = math.acos(cos_sim)
print math.degrees(angle_in_radians)
58.462437107432784</pre>
          <p>And that angle of ~58.5 is the angle between the first and the third document of our document set.</p>
         </div>
         <div></div>

          That is it, I hope you liked this third tutorial !
