      <p>The <b>Jaccard index</b>, also known as <b>Intersection over Union</b> and the <b>Jaccard similarity coefficient</b> (originally coined <i>coefficient de communauté</i> by <a href="/wiki/Paul_Jaccard" title="Paul Jaccard">Paul Jaccard</a>), is a <a href="/wiki/Statistic" title="Statistic">statistic</a> used for comparing the <a href="/wiki/Similarity_measure" title="Similarity measure">similarity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Diversity_index" title="Diversity index">diversity</a> of <a href="/wiki/Sample_(statistics)" title="Sample (statistics)">sample</a> sets. The Jaccard coefficient measures similarity between finite sample sets, and is defined as the size of the <a href="/wiki/Intersection_(set_theory)" title="Intersection (set theory)">intersection</a> divided by the size of the <a href="/wiki/Union_(set_theory)" title="Union (set theory)">union</a> of the sample sets:</p>

      <h3><span id="Difference_with_the_simple_matching_coefficient_(SMC)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Difference_with_the_simple_matching_coefficient_.28SMC.29">Difference with the simple matching coefficient (SMC)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jaccard_index&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Difference with the simple matching coefficient (SMC)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>When used for binary attributes, the Jaccard index is very similar to the <a href="/wiki/Simple_matching_coefficient" title="Simple matching coefficient">simple matching coefficient</a>. The main difference is that the SMC has the term <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;">
         <math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"> 
          <semantics> 
           <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> 
            <mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> 
             <msub> 
              <mi>
               M
              </mi> 
              <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> 
               <mn>
                00
               </mn> 
              </mrow> 
             </msub> 
            </mstyle> 
           </mrow> 
           <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">
            {\displaystyle M_{00}}
           </annotation> 
          </semantics> 
         </math></span><img src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e3e73b7d9853b5c1ec725d38277514d780a02c8a" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" aria-hidden="true" style="vertical-align: -0.671ex; width:4.156ex; height:2.509ex;" alt="M_{00}"></span> in its numerator and denominator, whereas the Jaccard index does not. Thus, the SMC counts both mutual presences (when an attribute is present in both sets) and mutual absence (when an attribute is absent in both sets) as matches and compares it to the total number of attributes in the universe, whereas the Jaccard index only counts mutual presence as matches and compares it to the number of attributes that have been chosen by at least one of the two sets.</p> 
      <p>In market basket analysis, for example, the basket of two consumers who we wish to compare might only contain a small fraction of all the available products in the store, so the SMC will usually return very high values of similarities even when the baskets bear very little resemblance, thus making the Jaccard index a more appropriate measure of similarity in that context. For example, consider a supermarket with 1000 products and two customers. The basket of the first customer contains salt and pepper and the basket of the second contains salt and sugar. In this scenario, the similarity between the two baskets as measured by the Jaccard index would be 1/3, but the similarity becomes 0.998 using the SMC.</p> 
      <p>In other contexts, where 0 and 1 carry equivalent information (symmetry), the SMC is a better measure of similarity. For example, vectors of demographic variables stored in <a href="/wiki/Dummy_variable_(statistics)" title="Dummy variable (statistics)">dummies variables</a>, such as gender, would be better compared with the SMC than with the Jaccard index since the impact of gender on similarity should be equal, independently of whether male is defined as a 0 and female as a 1 or the other way around. However, when we have symmetric dummy variables, one could replicate the behaviour of the SMC by splitting the dummies into two binary attributes (in this case, male and female), thus transforming them into asymmetric attributes, allowing the use of the Jaccard index without introducing any bias. By using this trick, the Jaccard index can be considered as making the SMC a fully redundant metric. The SMC remains, however, more computationally efficient in the case of symmetric dummy variables since it does not require adding extra dimensions.</p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tanimoto_similarity_and_distance">Tanimoto similarity and distance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jaccard_index&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Tanimoto similarity and distance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Various forms of functions described as Tanimoto similarity and Tanimoto distance occur in the literature and on the Internet. Most of these are synonyms for Jaccard similarity and Jaccard distance, but some are mathematically different. Many sources<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> cite an IBM Technical Report<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> as the seminal reference. The report is available from <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.worldcat.org/title/elementary-mathematical-theory-of-classification-and-prediction/oclc/10917698&amp;referer=brief_results">several libraries</a>.</p> 
      <p>In "A Computer Program for Classifying Plants", published in October 1960,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> a method of classification based on a similarity ratio, and a derived distance function, is given. It seems that this is the most authoritative source for the meaning of the terms "Tanimoto similarity" and "Tanimoto Distance". The similarity ratio is equivalent to Jaccard similarity, but the distance function is <i>not</i> the same as Jaccard distance.</p> 
      <h3><span id="Tanimoto's_definitions_of_similarity_and_distance"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Tanimoto.27s_definitions_of_similarity_and_distance">Tanimoto's definitions of similarity and distance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jaccard_index&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Tanimoto's definitions of similarity and distance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
