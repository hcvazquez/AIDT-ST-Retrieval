     <p><strong>Definition: </strong></p>
     <p>(1) The process of turning an original or "source" <a href="https://www.thoughtco.com/text-language-studies-1692537" data-component="link" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" data-ordinal="1">text</a> into a text in another <a href="https://www.thoughtco.com/what-is-a-language-1691218" data-component="link" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" data-ordinal="2">language</a>.</p>
     <p>(2) A translated version of a text.</p>
     <p>An individual or a computer program that renders a text into another language is called a <em>translator</em>. The discipline concerned with issues related to the production of translations is called <em>translation studies</em>. (See Examples and Observations, below.)</p>
     <p>See also:</p>

     </ul>

     <p><strong>Etymology:</strong><br>From the Latin, "transfer"</p>
     <h3>Examples and Observations:</h3>
     <ul>
      <li> <strong>Three Types of Translation</strong><br>"In his seminal paper, 'On Linguistic Aspects of Translation' (Jacobson 1959/2000. see Section B, Text B1.1), the Russo-American <a href="https://www.thoughtco.com/what-is-a-linguist-1691239" data-component="link" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" data-ordinal="6">linguist</a> Roman Jakobson makes a very important distinction between three types of written <strong>translation</strong>:<br>&nbsp;
       <ol>
        <li> <strong>intralingual translation</strong> - translation within the same language, which can involve rewording or <a href="https://www.thoughtco.com/what-is-a-paraphrase-1691573" data-component="link" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" data-ordinal="7">paraphrase</a>;</li>
        <li> <strong>interlingual translation</strong> - translation from one language to another, and</li>
        <li> <strong>intersemiotic translation</strong> - translation of the verbal <a href="https://www.thoughtco.com/sign-semiotics-1692096" data-component="link" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" data-ordinal="8">sign</a> by a non-verbal sign, for example music or image.</li>
       </ol>Only the second category, interlingual translation, is deemed 'translation proper' by Jakobson."<br>(Basil Hatim and Jeremy Munday, <em>Translation: An Advanced Resource Book</em>. Routledge, 2005)</li>
      <li>"<strong>Translation</strong> is like a woman. If it is beautiful, it is not faithful. If it is faithful, it is most certainly not beautiful."<br>(attributed to Yevgeny Yevtushenko, among others)</li>
     </ul> 
     <div id="native-placeholder_1-0" class="comp native-placeholder mntl-block"> 
     </div> 
     <!-- end: comp native-placeholder mntl-block --> 
     <ul class="cb-split">
      <li> <strong>Translation and Style</strong><br>"To translate, one must have a <a href="https://www.thoughtco.com/style-rhetoric-and-composition-1692148" data-component="link" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" data-ordinal="9">style</a> of his own, for otherwise the <strong>translation</strong> will have no <a href="https://www.thoughtco.com/rhythm-phonetics-poetics-and-style-1692065" data-component="link" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" data-ordinal="10">rhythm</a> or nuance, which come from the process of artistically thinking through and molding the sentences; they cannot be reconstituted by piecemeal imitation. The problem of translation is to retreat to a simpler tenor of one's own style and creatively adjust this to one's author."<br>(Paul Goodman, <em>Five Years: Thoughts During a Useless Time</em>, 1969)</li>
     </ul> 
     <div id="billboard2-sticky_1-0" class="comp billboard2-sticky billboard-sticky" data-height="600" data-parent>

     <ul class="cb-split">
      <li> <strong>The Illusion of Transparency</strong><br>"A translated text, whether prose or poetry, fiction or nonfiction, is judged acceptable by most publishers, reviewers, and readers when it reads fluently, when the absence of any linguistic or stylistic peculiarities makes it seem transparent, giving the appearance that it reflects the foreign writer's personality or intention or the essential <a href="https://www.thoughtco.com/meaning-semantics-term-1691373" data-component="link" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" data-ordinal="11">meaning</a> of the foreign text--the appearance, in other words, that the translation is not in fact a translation, but the 'original.' The illusion of transparency is an effect of fluent <a href="https://www.thoughtco.com/discourse-language-term-1690464" data-component="link" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" data-ordinal="12">discourse</a>, of the translator's effort to insure easy readability by adhering to current <a href="https://www.thoughtco.com/usage-grammar-1692575" data-component="link" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" data-ordinal="13">usage</a>, maintaining continuous <a href="https://www.thoughtco.com/syntax-grammar-1692182" data-component="link" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" data-ordinal="14">syntax</a>, fixing a precise meaning. What is so remarkable here is that this illusory effect conceals the numerous conditions under which the <strong>translation</strong> is made . . .."<br>(Lawrence Venuti, <em>The Translator's Invisibility: A History of Translation</em>. Routledge, 1995)</li>
      <li> <strong>The Process of Translation</strong><br>"Here, then, is the full process of <strong>translation</strong>. At one point we have a writer in a room, struggling to approximate the impossible vision that hovers over his head. He finishes it, with misgivings. Some time later we have a translator struggling to approximate the vision, not to mention the particulars of language and voice, of the text that lies before him. He does the best he can, but is never satisfied. And then, finally, we have the reader. The reader is the least tortured of this trio, but the reader too may very well feel that he is missing something in the book, that through sheer ineptitude he is failing to be a proper vessel for the book’s overarching vision."<br>(Michael Cunningham, "Found in Translation." <em>The New York Times</em>, Oct. 2, 2010)</li>
     </ul> 
     <ul class="cb-split">
      <li> <strong>The Untranslatable</strong><br>"Just as there are no exact <a href="https://www.thoughtco.com/synonym-definition-1692177" data-component="link" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" data-ordinal="15">synonyms</a> within a language ('big' does not mean precisely the same as 'large'), there are no exact matches for words or expressions across languages. I can express the notion 'four year old male uncastrated domesticated reindeer' in English. But our tongue lacks the economy of information packaging found in Tofa, a nearly extinct tongue I studied in Siberia. Tofa equips reindeer herders with words like 'chary' with the above meaning. Furthermore, that word exists within a multidimensional matrix that defines the four salient (for the Tofa people) parameters of reindeer: age, sex, fertility, and rideability. Words are untranslateable because [they] do not exist in a flat, alphabetised <a href="https://www.thoughtco.com/what-is-a-dictionary-1690450" data-component="link" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" data-ordinal="16">dictionary</a> style list, but rather in a richly structured taxonomy of <a href="https://www.thoughtco.com/meaning-semantics-term-1691373" data-component="link" data-source="inlineLink" data-type="internalLink" data-ordinal="17">meaning</a>. They are defined by their oppositions to and similarities to multiple other words--in other words, the cultural backdrop."<br>(K. David Harrison, linguist at Swarthmore College, in "Seven Questions for K. David Harrison." <em>The Economist</em>, Nov. 23, 2010)</li>
     </ul>
