          <div class="postText ">
           You can't. You can check whether an email is syntactically valid, but the email servers for most domains will reject an attempt to check whether any particular email exists. And even if the address exists, it may still be that the account is no longer checked by its user, or doesn't accept mail because it's full, or has been deactivated, or doesn't accept mails from your server because the sending domain/server doesn't validate in some way. 
           <br> 
           <br> In short: there are any number of reasons why an email might not go through that you can't check without sending an email to that address and have the user act on it.

          <div class="postText ">
           No, it is not possible, as Ulf said. Doesn't matter if you use Spring or not. It's not a matter of using the right framework or library. It's just not possible in principle. 
           <br> 
           <br> The whole reason that websites send verification e-mails is because there's no other way to verify if an e-mail address is a real e-mail address that works. And even that isn't fool-proof; people can make temporary e-mail addresses that get deleted after they've answered the verification e-mail. There's just no 100% fool-proof way to check if an e-mail address is valid and real, certainly not if you don't want to send a verification e-mail.

           Let's try to clarify that. 
           <br> 
           <br> 1. You ask the user for an e-mail address.

             <cite>Eshwara Chaluvaiah wrote:</cite>i read it, but if email does not exist means it will bounce back to our email...
            </div> 
           </blockquote> 
           <br> You may have read it, but you're not 
           <i>listening</i>. 
           <br> 
           <br> I was a systems administrator for 15 years and, to the best of my knowledge, 
           <i>there is NO reliable way to know if an e-mail address exists without requiring a <u>response</u> from it</i>. 
           <br> 
           <br> And the way to do that - as Paul has already told you - is to make it 
           <i>your user's responsibility</i> to provide you with a valid 
           <u>and accessible</u> e-mail address. 
           <br> 
           <br> And the best way to do 
           <i>that</i> - and the one used by most websites I know - is to send an "activation URL" (or code) to that e-mail address. 
           <br> 
           <br> But even that only ensures that the address was OK 
           <i>at the time of activation</i>. There's nothing to stop your "user" from deleting it afterwards.
