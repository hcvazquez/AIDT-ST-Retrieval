        <div class="sect2" title="Problem">
         <div class="titlepage">
          <div>
           <div>
            <h2 class="title"><a id="validation-email-problem"></a>Problem</h2>
           </div>
          </div>
         </div>
         <p>You have a form on your website or a dialog box in your application that asks the user for an email address. You want to use a regular expression to validate this email address before trying to send email to it. This reduces the number of emails returned to you as undeliverable.<a id="I_indexterm4_d1e26632" class="indexterm"></a></p>

It only validates that the string contains an at sign (@) that is preceded and followed by one or more nonwhitespace characters.<a id="I_indexterm4_d1e26666" class="indexterm"></a><a id="I_indexterm4_d1e26667" class="indexterm"></a></p>

)*@[A-Z0-9-]+(?:\.[A-Z0-9-]+)*\Z</pre>

         <div class="sect3" title="Top-level domain has two to six letters">
          <div class="titlepage">
           <div>
            <div>
             <h3 class="title"><a id="validation-email-solution-tld"></a>Top-level domain has two to six letters</h3>
            </div>
           </div>
          </div>
          <p><a id="idx10687" class="indexterm"></a><a id="idx10708" class="indexterm"></a>This regular expression adds to the previous versions by specifying that the domain name must include at least one dot, and that the part of the domain name after the last dot can only consist of letters. That is, the domain must contain at least two levels, such as <code class="literal">secondlevel.com</code> or <code class="literal">thirdlevel.secondlevel.com</code>. The top-level domain (<a id="idx10558" class="indexterm"></a><code class="literal">.com</code> in these examples) must consist of two to six letters. All country-code top-level domains (<a id="I_indexterm4_d1e26856" class="indexterm"></a><code class="literal">.us</code>, <a id="I_indexterm4_d1e26863" class="indexterm"></a><code class="literal">.uk</code>, etc.) have two letters. The generic top-level domains have between three (<code class="literal">.com</code>) and six letters (<code class="literal">.museum</code>):<a id="I_indexterm4_d1e26875" class="indexterm"></a><a id="I_indexterm4_d1e26876" class="indexterm"></a></p>
          <a id="I_programlisting4_d1e21324"></a>
          <pre class="programlisting">^[\w!#$%&amp;'*+/=?`{|}~^-]+(?:\.[\w!#$%&amp;'*+/=?`{|}~^-]+)*@?
(?:[A-Z0-9-]+\.)+[A-Z]{2,6}$</pre>

        <div class="sect2" title="Discussion">
         <div class="titlepage">
          <div>
           <div>
            <h2 class="title"><a id="validation-email-discussion"></a>Discussion</h2>
           </div>
          </div>
         </div>
         <div class="sect3" title="About email addresses">
          <div class="titlepage">
           <div>
            <div>
             <h3 class="title"><a id="validation-email-discussion-email"></a>About email addresses</h3>
            </div>
           </div>
          </div>
          <p><a id="idx10593" class="indexterm"></a><a id="idx10705" class="indexterm"></a>If you thought something as conceptually simple as validating an email address would have a simple one-size-fits-all regex solution, you’re quite wrong. This recipe is a prime example that before you can start writing a regular expression, you have to decide <span class="italic">exactly</span> what you want to match. There is no universally agreed-upon rule as to which email addresses are valid and which not. It depends on your definition of <span class="italic">valid</span>.</p>
          <p><code class="userinput">asdf@asdf.asdf</code> is valid according to <a id="I_indexterm4_d1e26929" class="indexterm"></a>RFC 5322, which defines the syntax for email addresses. But it is not valid if your definition specifies that a valid email address is one that accepts mail. There is no top-level <code class="userinput">asdf</code> domain.</p>
          <p>The short answer to the validity problem is that you can’t know whether <code class="userinput">john.doe@somewhere.com</code> is an email address that can actually receive email until you try to send email to it. And even then, you can’t be sure if the lack of response signals that the <code class="userinput">somewhere.com</code> domain is silently discarding mail sent to nonexistent mailboxes, or if John Doe hit the Delete button on his keyboard, or if his spam filter beat him to it.</p>
          <p>Because you ultimately have to check whether the address exists by actually sending email to it, you can decide to use a simpler or more relaxed regular expression. Allowing invalid addresses to slip through may be preferable to annoying people by blocking valid addresses. For this reason, you may want to select the “simple” regular expression. Though it obviously allows many things that aren’t email addresses, such as <code class="underline">#$%@.-</code>, the regex is quick and simple, and will never block a valid email address.</p>
          <p>If you want to avoid sending too many undeliverable emails, while still not blocking any real email addresses, the regex in <a class="xref" href="ch04s01.html#validation-email-solution-tld" title="Top-level domain has two to six letters">Top-level domain has two to six letters</a> is a good choice.</p>
          <p>You have to consider how complex you want your regular expression to be. If you’re validating user input, you’ll likely want a more complex regex, because the user could type in anything. But if you’re scanning database files that you know contain only valid email addresses, you can use a very simple regex that merely separates the email addresses from the other data. Even the solution in the earlier subsection <a class="xref" href="ch04s01.html#validation-email-solution-simple" title="Simple"></a> may be enough in this case.</p>
          <p>Finally, you have to consider how future-proof you want your regular expression to be. In the past, it made sense to restrict the top-level domain to only two-letter combinations for the country codes, and exhaustively list the generic top-level domains—that is, <span class="gray50">‹</span><code class="literal">com|net|org|mil|edu</code><span class="gray50">›</span>. With new top-level domains being added all the time, such regular expressions now quickly go out of date.</p>
         </div>
         <div class="sect3" title="Regular expression syntax">
          <div class="titlepage">
           <div>
            <div>
             <h3 class="title"><a id="validation-email-discussion-regex"></a>Regular expression syntax</h3>
            </div>
           </div>
          </div>
          <p>The regular expressions presented in this recipe show all the basic parts of the regular expression syntax in action. If you read up on these parts in <a class="xref" href="ch02.html" title="Chapter&nbsp;2.&nbsp;Basic Regular Expression Skills">Chapter&nbsp;2</a>, you can already do 90% of the jobs that are best solved with regular expressions.</p>
          <p>All the regular expressions, except the “simple” one, require the case-insensitive matching option to be turned on. Otherwise, only uppercase characters will be allowed. Turning on this option allows you to type <span class="gray50">‹</span><code class="literal">[A-Z]</code><span class="gray50">›</span> instead of <span class="gray50">‹</span><code class="literal">[A-Za-z]</code><span class="gray50">›</span>, saving a few keystrokes.</p>
          <p><a id="I_indexterm4_d1e26979" class="indexterm"></a><span class="gray50">‹</span><code class="literal">\S</code><span class="gray50">›</span> is a shorthand character class, as <a class="xref" href="ch02s03.html" title="2.3.&nbsp;Match One of Many Characters">Recipe&nbsp;2.3</a> explains. <span class="gray50">‹</span><code class="literal">\S</code><span class="gray50">›</span> matches any character that is not a whitespace character.</p>
          <p><span class="gray50">‹</span><code class="literal">@</code><span class="gray50">›</span> and <span class="gray50">‹</span><code class="literal">\.</code><span class="gray50">›</span> match a literal @ sign and a dot, respectively. Since the dot is a metacharacter when used outside character classes, it needs to be escaped with a backslash. The @ sign never has a special meaning with any of the regular expression flavors in this book. <a class="xref" href="ch02s01.html" title="2.1.&nbsp;Match Literal Text">Recipe&nbsp;2.1</a> gives you a list of all the metacharacters that need to be escaped.</p>
          <p><span class="gray50">‹</span><code class="literal">[A-Z0-9.-]</code><span class="gray50">›</span> and the other sequences between square brackets are character classes. This one allows all letters between A and Z, all digits between 0 and 9, as well as a literal dot and hyphen. Though the hyphen normally creates a range in a character class, the hyphen is treated as a literal when it occurs as the first or last character in a character class. <a class="xref" href="ch02s03.html" title="2.3.&nbsp;Match One of Many Characters">Recipe&nbsp;2.3</a> tells you all about character classes, including combining them with shorthands, as in <span class="gray50">‹</span><code class="literal">[A-Z0-9_!#$%&amp;'*+/=?`{|}~^.-]</code><span class="gray50">›</span>. This class matches a word character, as well as any of the 19 listed punctuation characters.</p>
          <p><a id="I_indexterm4_d1e27009" class="indexterm"></a><span class="gray50">‹</span><code class="literal">+</code><span class="gray50">›</span> and <a id="I_indexterm4_d1e27015" class="indexterm"></a><span class="gray50">‹</span><code class="literal">*</code><span class="gray50">›</span>, when used outside character classes, are quantifiers. The plus sign repeats the preceding regex token one or more times, whereas the asterisk repeats it zero or more times. In these regular expressions, the quantified token is usually a character class, and sometimes a group. Therefore, <span class="gray50">‹</span><code class="literal">[A-Z0-9.-]+</code><span class="gray50">›</span> matches one or more letters, digits, dots, and/or hyphens.</p>
          <p>As an example of the use of a group, <span class="gray50">‹</span><code class="literal">(?:[A-Z0-9-]+\.)+</code><span class="gray50">›</span> matches one or more letters, digits, and/or hyphens, followed by one literal dot. The plus sign repeats this group one or more times. The group must match at least once, but can match as many times as possible. <a class="xref" href="ch02s12.html" title="2.12.&nbsp;Repeat Part of the Regex a Certain Number of Times">Recipe&nbsp;2.12</a> explains the mechanics of the plus sign and other quantifiers in detail.</p>
          <p><a id="I_indexterm4_d1e27032" class="indexterm"></a><a id="I_indexterm4_d1e27035" class="indexterm"></a><span class="gray50">‹</span><code class="literal">(?:<span class="gray50">?</span>)</code><span class="gray50">›</span> is a noncapturing group. The capturing group <span class="gray50">‹</span><code class="literal">(<span class="gray50">?</span>)</code><span class="gray50">›</span> does the same thing with a cleaner syntax, so you could replace <a id="I_indexterm4_d1e27050" class="indexterm"></a><span class="gray50">‹</span><code class="literal">(?:</code><span class="gray50">›</span> with <span class="gray50">‹</span><code class="literal">(</code><span class="gray50">›</span> in all of the regular expressions we’ve used so far without changing the overall match results. But since we’re not interested in separately capturing parts of the email address, the noncapturing group is somewhat more efficient, although it makes the regular expression somewhat harder to read. <a class="xref" href="ch02s09.html" title="2.9.&nbsp;Group and Capture Parts of the Match">Recipe&nbsp;2.9</a> tells you all about capturing and noncapturing groups.</p>
          <p>In most regex flavors, the anchors <a id="I_indexterm4_d1e27064" class="indexterm"></a><span class="gray50">‹</span><code class="literal">^</code><span class="gray50">›</span> and <span class="gray50">‹</span><code class="literal">$</code><span class="gray50">›</span> force the regular expression to find its match at the start and end of the subject text, respectively. Placing the whole regular expression between these characters effectively requires the regular expression to match the entire subject.</p>
          <p>This is important when validating user input. You do not want to accept <code class="userinput">drop database; </code><code class="userinput">-- joe@server.com haha!</code> as a valid email address. Without the anchors, all the previous regular expressions will match because they find <code class="underline">joe@server.com</code> in the middle of the given text. See <a class="xref" href="ch02s05.html" title="2.5.&nbsp;Match Something at the Start and/or the End of a Line">Recipe&nbsp;2.5</a> for details about anchors. That recipe also explains why the “^ and $ match at line breaks” matching option must be off for these regular expressions.</p>
          <p>In Ruby, the caret and dollar always match at line breaks. The regular expressions using the caret and dollar work correctly in Ruby, but only if the string you’re trying to validate contains no line breaks. If the string may contain line breaks, all the regexes using <span class="gray50">‹</span><code class="literal">^</code><span class="gray50">›</span> and <span class="gray50">‹</span><code class="literal">$</code><span class="gray50">›</span> will match the email address in <code class="userinput">drop database; -- <code class="keycap">LF</code>joe@server.com<code class="keycap">LF</code> haha!</code>, where <code class="keycap">LF</code> represents a line break.</p>
          <p><a id="I_indexterm4_d1e27111" class="indexterm"></a>To avoid this, use the anchors <span class="gray50">‹</span><code class="literal">\A</code><span class="gray50">›</span> and <a id="I_indexterm4_d1e27120" class="indexterm"></a><span class="gray50">‹</span><code class="literal">\Z</code><span class="gray50">›</span> instead. These match at the start and end of the string only, regardless of any options, in all flavors discussed in this book, except JavaScript. JavaScript does not support <span class="gray50">‹</span><code class="literal">\A</code><span class="gray50">›</span> and <span class="gray50">‹</span><code class="literal">\Z</code><span class="gray50">›</span> at all. <a class="xref" href="ch02s05.html" title="2.5.&nbsp;Match Something at the Start and/or the End of a Line">Recipe&nbsp;2.5</a> explains these anchors.</p>
          <div class="caution" title="Caution">
           <h3 class="title">Caution</h3>
           <p>The issue with <a id="I_indexterm4_d1e27140" class="indexterm"></a><a id="I_indexterm4_d1e27145" class="indexterm"></a><span class="gray50">‹</span><code class="literal">^</code><span class="gray50">›</span> and <span class="gray50">‹</span><code class="literal">$</code><span class="gray50">›</span> versus <span class="gray50">‹</span><code class="literal">\A</code><span class="gray50">›</span> and <span class="gray50">‹</span><code class="literal">\Z</code><span class="gray50">›</span> applies to all regular expressions that validate input. There are a lot of these in this book. Although we will offer the occasional reminder, we will not constantly repeat this advice or show separate solutions for JavaScript and Ruby for each and every recipe. In many cases, we’ll show only one solution using the caret and dollar, and list Ruby as a compatible flavor. If you’re using Ruby, remember to use <span class="gray50">‹</span><code class="literal">\A</code><span class="gray50">›</span> and <span class="gray50">‹</span><code class="literal">\Z</code><span class="gray50">›</span> if you want to avoid matching one line in a multiline string.</p>
          </div>
         </div>
         <div class="sect3" title="Building a regex step-by-step">
          <div class="titlepage">
           <div>
            <div>
             <h3 class="title"><a id="validation-email-discussion-building"></a>Building a regex step-by-step</h3>
            </div>
           </div>
          </div>
          <p>This recipe illustrates how you can build a regular expression step-by-step. This technique is particularly handy with an interactive regular expression tester, such as RegexBuddy.</p>
          <p>First, load a bunch of valid and invalid sample data into the tool. In this case, that would be a list of valid email addresses and a list of invalid email addresses.</p>
          <p>Then, write a simple regular expression that matches all the valid email addresses. Ignore the invalid addresses for now. <span class="gray50">‹</span><code class="literal">^\S+@\S+$</code><span class="gray50">›</span> already defines the basic structure of an email address: a local part, an at sign, and a domain name.</p>
          <p>With the basic structure of your text pattern defined, you can refine each part until your regular expression no longer matches any of the invalid data. If your regular expression only has to work with previously existing data, that can be a quick job. If your regex has to work with any user input, editing the regular expression until it is restrictive enough will be a much harder job than just getting it to match the valid data.<a id="I_indexterm4_d1e27186" class="indexterm"></a><a id="I_indexterm4_d1e27187" class="indexterm"></a></p>
         </div>
        </div>
        <div class="sect2" title="Variations">
         <div class="titlepage">
          <div>
           <div>
            <h2 class="title"><a id="validation-email-variations"></a>Variations</h2>
           </div>
          </div>
         </div>
         <p>If you want to search for email addresses in larger bodies of text instead of checking whether the input as a whole is an email address, you cannot use the anchors <span class="gray50">‹</span><code class="literal">^</code><span class="gray50">›</span> and <span class="gray50">‹</span><code class="literal">$</code><span class="gray50">›</span>. Merely removing the anchors from the regular expression is not the right solution. If you do that with the final regex, which restricts the top-level domain to letters, it will match <code class="underline">john@doe.com</code> in <code class="userinput">john@doe.com77</code>, for example. Instead of anchoring the regex match to the start and end of the subject, you have to specify that the start of the local part and the top-level domain cannot be part of longer words.</p>
         <p>This is easily done with a pair of word boundaries. Replace both <span class="gray50">‹</span><code class="literal">^</code><span class="gray50">›</span> and <span class="gray50">‹</span><code class="literal">$</code><span class="gray50">›</span> <span>with <span class="gray50">‹</span><code class="literal">\b</code><span class="gray50">›</span>. For</span> instance, <span class="gray50">‹</span><code class="literal">^[A-Z0-9+_.-]+@[A-Z0-9.-]+$</code><span class="gray50">›</span> becomes <span><span class="gray50">‹</span><code class="literal">\b[A-Z0-9+_.-]+@[A-Z0-9.-]+\b</code><span class="gray50">›</span>.</span><a id="I_indexterm4_d1e27229" class="indexterm"></a><a id="I_indexterm4_d1e27231" class="indexterm"></a></p>
        </div>
        <div class="sect2" title="See Also">
         <div class="titlepage">
          <div>
           <div>
            <h2 class="title"><a id="validation-email-seealso"></a>See Also</h2>
           </div>
          </div>
         </div>
         <p><a id="I_indexterm4_d1e27236" class="indexterm"></a>RFC 5322 defines the structure and syntax of email messages, including the email addresses used in email messages. You can download RFC 5322 at <a class="ulink" href="http://www.ietf.org/html/rfc5322.txt">http://www.ietf.org/html/rfc5322.txt</a>.</p>

         <p>Techniques used in the regular expressions in this recipe are discussed in <a class="xref" href="ch02.html" title="Chapter&nbsp;2.&nbsp;Basic Regular Expression Skills">Chapter&nbsp;2</a>. <a class="xref" href="ch02s01.html" title="2.1.&nbsp;Match Literal Text">Recipe&nbsp;2.1</a> explains which special characters need to be escaped. <a class="xref" href="ch02s03.html" title="2.3.&nbsp;Match One of Many Characters">Recipe&nbsp;2.3</a> explains character classes. <a class="xref" href="ch02s05.html" title="2.5.&nbsp;Match Something at the Start and/or the End of a Line">Recipe&nbsp;2.5</a> explains anchors. <a class="xref" href="ch02s06.html" title="2.6.&nbsp;Match Whole Words">Recipe&nbsp;2.6</a> explains word boundaries. <a class="xref" href="ch02s09.html" title="2.9.&nbsp;Group and Capture Parts of the Match">Recipe&nbsp;2.9</a> explains grouping. <a class="xref" href="ch02s12.html" title="2.12.&nbsp;Repeat Part of the Regex a Certain Number of Times">Recipe&nbsp;2.12</a> explains repetition.</p>
