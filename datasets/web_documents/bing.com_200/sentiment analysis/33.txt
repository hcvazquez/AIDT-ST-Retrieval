       <p>Text <b>sentiment analysis</b> (or usually just <i>sentiment analysis</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Text_mining" title="Text mining">text mining</a> technique to analyze the sentiment of the writer or to the topic written about. </p>
       <p><a href="/wiki/Bo_Pang" title="Bo Pang">Bo Pang</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lillian_Lee&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Lillian Lee (page does not exist)">Lillian Lee</a> have written a lengthy introduction to sentiment analysis: <i><a href="/wiki/Opinion_mining_and_sentiment_analysis" title="Opinion mining and sentiment analysis">Opinion mining and sentiment analysis</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-0">[1]</a></sup> </p>
       <p>Sentiment analysis may be combined with another text-mining technique, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Topic_mining&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Topic mining (page does not exist)">topic mining</a>, in what is called <a href="/w/index.php?title=Topic-sentiment_analysis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Topic-sentiment analysis (page does not exist)">topic-sentiment analysis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[2]</a></sup> </p>

       <h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Sentiment_analysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Methods">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Methods"> Methods </span></h2> 
       <p>Sentiment analysis may employ <a href="/wiki/Machine_learning" title="Machine learning">machine learning</a> techniques. One often apply method is <a href="/w/index.php?title=Na%C3%AFve_Bayes_classifier&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Naïve Bayes classifier (page does not exist)">naïve Bayes classifier</a> where the algorithm is trained on a labeled data set. Within the <a href="/wiki/Python" title="Python">Python</a> package <a href="/wiki/NLTK" title="NLTK">NLTK</a> is a classic sentiment analysis data set (movie reviews) as well as general machine learning methods for sentiment classification. Some of the earliest papers on this approach are probably </p>
       <p>Another approach is to use a word list where each word has been scored for positivity/negativity or sentiment strength. There exists several word lists: <a href="/wiki/ANEW" title="ANEW">ANEW</a> is the oldest and has around 1000 words, <a href="/wiki/AFINN" title="AFINN">AFINN</a> is newer and has around 2.500, while <a href="/wiki/LabMT" title="LabMT">labMT</a> has over 10.000 words scored. </p>
       <p>One way to extended word lists is to use word co-occurence or a word ontology such as <a href="/wiki/WordNet" title="WordNet">WordNet</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[3]</a></sup> The method may go back to 1957.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[4]</a></sup> </p>

       <h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Sentiment_analysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Affective word lists">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Affective_word_lists"> Affective word lists </span></h2> 
       <p>Sentiment analysis may use word lists annotated for their arousal and their valence, i.e., whether they are positive or negative. Some word lists are listed and commented on in setion 7.3 of the Pang/Lee monograph. Some of the word lists are: </p> 
       <dl>
        <dt>
          Affective Norms for English Words (
         <a href="/wiki/ANEW" title="ANEW">ANEW</a>)&nbsp;
        </dt>
        <dd>
          An English word list constructed by Bradley and Lang
         <sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[9]</a></sup> and available from 
         <a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Florida&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="University of Florida (page does not exist)">University of Florida</a> 
         <a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://csea.phhp.ufl.edu/media/anewmessage.html">[12]</a>. There are 1034 words rated for 
         <a href="/w/index.php?title=Valence&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Valence (page does not exist)">valence</a>,

       <h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Sentiment_analysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Evaluation">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Evaluation"> Evaluation </span></h2> 
       <p>Performance of a sentiment analysis system may depend on corpus and of annotation. Annotation may be a sentiment strength for each text or an categorical variable, 2-class: positive/negative, 3-class: positive/negative/neutral or 4-class: positive/negative/both/neutral.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[32]</a></sup> </p>
       <p>Sentiment analysis performance of humans have ben reported to be 82-90%.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[33]</a></sup> </p>
