   <h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">OAuth</h1> 
   <div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content"> 
    <div id="siteSub" class="noprint">

      <p><b>OAuth</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Open_standard" title="Open standard">open standard</a> for access delegation, commonly used as a way for Internet users to grant websites or applications access to their information on other websites but without giving them the passwords.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> This mechanism is used by companies such as Google, Facebook, Microsoft and Twitter to permit the users to share information about their accounts with third party applications or websites.</p> 
      <p>Generally, OAuth provides to clients a "secure delegated access" to server resources on behalf of a resource owner. It specifies a process for resource owners to authorize third-party access to their server resources without sharing their credentials. Designed specifically to work with <a href="/wiki/Hypertext_Transfer_Protocol" title="Hypertext Transfer Protocol">Hypertext Transfer Protocol</a> (HTTP), OAuth essentially allows <a href="/wiki/Access_token" title="Access token">access tokens</a> to be issued to third-party clients by an authorization server, with the approval of the resource owner. The third party then uses the access token to access the protected resources hosted by the resource server.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p> 
      <p>OAuth is a service that is complementary to and distinct from <a href="/wiki/OpenID" title="OpenID">OpenID</a>. OAuth is also distinct from <a href="/wiki/Initiative_For_Open_Authentication" title="Initiative For Open Authentication">OATH</a>, which is a <i>reference architecture</i> for <i>authentication</i>, not a <i>standard</i> for <i>authorization</i>. However, OAuth is directly related to <a href="/wiki/OpenID_Connect" title="OpenID Connect">OpenID Connect (OIDC)</a> since OIDC is an authentication layer built on top of OAuth 2.0. OAuth is also distinct from <a href="/wiki/XACML" title="XACML">XACML</a>, which is an authorization policy standard. OAuth can be used in conjunction with <a href="/wiki/XACML" title="XACML">XACML</a> where OAuth is used for ownership consent and access delegation whereas <a href="/wiki/XACML" title="XACML">XACML</a> is used to define the authorization policies (e.g. managers can view documents in their region).</p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=OAuth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>OAuth began in November 2006 when <a href="/wiki/Blaine_Cook_(programmer)" title="Blaine Cook (programmer)">Blaine Cook</a> was developing the <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">Twitter</a> <a href="/wiki/OpenID" title="OpenID">OpenID</a> implementation. Meanwhile, <a href="/wiki/Ma.gnolia" class="mw-redirect" title="Ma.gnolia">Ma.gnolia</a> needed a solution to allow its members with OpenIDs to authorize <a href="/wiki/Dashboard_(Mac_OS)" class="mw-redirect" title="Dashboard (Mac OS)">Dashboard Widgets</a> to access their service. Cook, <a href="/wiki/Chris_Messina_(open_source_advocate)" class="mw-redirect" title="Chris Messina (open source advocate)">Chris Messina</a> and Larry Halff from Magnolia met with <a href="/wiki/David_Recordon" title="David Recordon">David Recordon</a> to discuss using OpenID with the Twitter and Ma.gnolia APIs to delegate authentication. They concluded that there were no <a href="/wiki/Open_standard" title="Open standard">open standards</a> for API access delegation.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p> 
      <p>The OAuth <a href="/wiki/Discussion_group" title="Discussion group">discussion group</a> was created in April 2007, for the small group of implementers to write the draft proposal for an open protocol. DeWitt Clinton from <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">Google</a> learned of the OAuth project, and expressed his interest in supporting the effort. In July 2007, the team drafted an initial specification. <a href="/w/index.php?title=Eran_Hammer&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Eran Hammer (page does not exist)">Eran Hammer</a> joined and coordinated the many OAuth contributions creating a more formal specification. On December 4, 2007, the OAuth Core 1.0 final draft was released.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p> 
      <p>At the 73rd Internet Engineering Task Force (IETF) meeting in <a href="/wiki/Minneapolis" title="Minneapolis">Minneapolis</a> in November 2008, an OAuth <a href="/wiki/Birds_of_a_feather_(computing)" title="Birds of a feather (computing)">BoF</a> was held to discuss bringing the protocol into the IETF for further standardization work. The event was well attended and there was wide support for formally chartering an OAuth working group within the IETF.</p> 
      <p>The OAuth 1.0 protocol was published as <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" rel="nofollow" href="//tools.ietf.org/html/rfc5849">RFC 5849</a>, an informational <a href="/wiki/Request_for_Comments" title="Request for Comments">Request for Comments</a>, in April 2010.</p> 
      <p>Since August 31, 2010, all third party Twitter applications have been required to use OAuth.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p> 
      <p>The OAuth 2.0 framework was published as <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" rel="nofollow" href="//tools.ietf.org/html/rfc6749">RFC 6749</a>, and the Bearer Token Usage as <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" rel="nofollow" href="//tools.ietf.org/html/rfc6750">RFC 6750</a>, both standards track <a href="/wiki/Requests_for_Comments" class="mw-redirect" title="Requests for Comments">Requests for Comments</a>, in October 2012.</p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="OAuth_2.0">OAuth 2.0</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=OAuth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: OAuth 2.0">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>OAuth 2.0 is not backwards compatible with OAuth 1.0. OAuth 2.0 provides specific authorization flows for web applications, desktop applications, mobile phones, and living room devices. The specification and associated RFCs are developed by the IETF OAuth WG;<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> the main framework was published in October 2012.</p> 
      <p><a href="/wiki/Facebook" title="Facebook">Facebook</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Facebook_Platform#Graph_API" title="Facebook Platform">Graph API</a> only supports OAuth 2.0.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">Google</a> supports OAuth 2.0 as the recommended authorization mechanism for all of its APIs.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> also supports OAuth 2.0 for various APIs and its Azure Active Directory service, which is used to secure many Microsoft and third party APIs.</p> 
      <p>The OAuth 2.0 Framework<sup id="cite_ref-Dick_Hardt_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dick_Hardt-9">[9]</a></sup> and Bearer Token Usage<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> were published in October 2012.</p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Security">Security</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=OAuth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Security">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>On April 23, 2009, a <a href="/wiki/Session_fixation" title="Session fixation">session fixation</a> security flaw in the 1.0 protocol was announced. It affects the OAuth authorization flow (also known as "3-legged OAuth") in OAuth Core 1.0 Section 6.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Version 1.0a of the OAuth Core protocol was issued to address this issue.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p> 
      <p>OAuth 2.0 does not support signature, encryption, channel binding, or client verification. It relies completely on <a href="/wiki/Transport_Layer_Security" title="Transport Layer Security">TLS</a> for some degree of confidentiality and server authentication.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p> 
      <p>OAuth 2.0 has had numerous security flaws exposed in implementations.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The protocol itself has been described as inherently insecure by security experts and a primary contributor to the specification stated that implementation mistakes are almost inevitable.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p> 
      <p>In January 2013, the Internet Engineering Task Force published a number of threat models for OAuth 2.0.<sup id="cite_ref-rfc_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rfc-18">[18]</a></sup> Among them was one called "Open Redirector"; in the spring of 2014, this was described under the name "Covert Redirect" by Wang Jing.<sup id="cite_ref-OAuth_Covert_Redirect_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OAuth_Covert_Redirect-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CNET_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CNET-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-PhysOrg_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PhysOrg-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Covert_Redirect_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Covert_Redirect-22">[22]</a></sup></p> 
      <p>Possibly the most devastating OAuth security failure is <a href="/wiki/Phishing" title="Phishing">phishing</a> vulnerability:<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> every web site using OAuth is visually (but not technically) asking end users for their username and password of their master identity, which prevents ordinary users from understanding that they should not type those in should they encounter an attacker's web site that visually emulates this process to steal credentials. Traditional two-factor authentication (using <a href="/wiki/One-time_password" title="One-time password">One-time passwords</a>) does not prevent this attack, because the phishing site can steal that as well, using it immediately (Note that <a href="/wiki/Universal_2nd_Factor" title="Universal 2nd Factor">Universal 2nd Factor</a> tokens aren't vulnerable to this specific type attack).</p> 
      <p>In April–May 2017, about one million users of <a href="/wiki/Gmail" title="Gmail">Gmail</a> (less than 0.1% of users as of May 2017) were targeted by an OAuth-based phishing attack, receiving an email purporting to be from a colleague, employer or friend wanting to share a document on Google Docs.<sup id="cite_ref-bbc1_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc1-24">[24]</a></sup> Those who clicked on the link within the email were directed to sign in and allow a potentially malicious third-party program called "Google Apps" access their "email account, contacts and online documents".<sup id="cite_ref-bbc1_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc1-24">[24]</a></sup> Within "approximately one hour",<sup id="cite_ref-bbc1_24-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc1-24">[24]</a></sup> the phishing attack was stopped by Google, who advised those who had given "Google Apps" access to their email to revoke such access and change their passwords.</p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-interoperability">Non-interoperability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=OAuth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Non-interoperability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Because OAuth 2.0 is more of a framework than a defined protocol, one OAuth 2.0 implementation is less likely to be naturally interoperable with another OAuth 2.0 implementation. Further deployment profiling and specification is required for any interoperability.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=OAuth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

      <p>OAuth can be used as an authorizing mechanism to consume secured <a href="/wiki/RSS" title="RSS">RSS</a>/<a href="/wiki/Atom_(standard)" title="Atom (standard)">ATOM</a> feeds. Consumption of RSS/ATOM feeds that require authentication has always been an issue. For example, an RSS feed from a secured <a href="/wiki/Google_Sites" title="Google Sites">Google Site</a> cannot be consumed using <a href="/wiki/Google_Reader" title="Google Reader">Google Reader</a>. Instead, three-legged OAuth can be used to authorize Google Reader to access the RSS feed from that Google Site.</p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="OAuth_and_other_standards">OAuth and other standards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=OAuth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: OAuth and other standards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="OpenID_vs._pseudo-authentication_using_OAuth">OpenID vs. pseudo-authentication using OAuth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=OAuth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: OpenID vs. pseudo-authentication using OAuth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>OAuth is an <i>authorization</i> protocol, rather than an <i>authentication</i> protocol. Using OAuth on its own as an authentication method may be referred to as pseudo-authentication.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The following diagrams highlight the differences between using <a href="/wiki/OpenID" title="OpenID">OpenID</a> (specifically designed as an authentication protocol) and OAuth for authentication.</p> 
      <p>The communication flow in both processes is similar:</p> 
      <ol> 
       <li>(Not pictured) The user requests a resource or site login from the application.</li> 
       <li>The site sees that the user is not authenticated. It formulates a request for the identity provider, encodes it, and sends it to the user as part of a redirect URL.</li> 
       <li>The user's browser requests the redirect URL for the identity provider, including the application's request</li> 
       <li>If necessary, the identity provider authenticates the user (perhaps by asking them for their username and password)</li> 
       <li>Once the identity provider is satisfied that the user is sufficiently authenticated, it processes the application's request, formulates a response, and sends that back to the user along with a redirect URL back to the application.</li> 
       <li>The user's browser requests the redirect URL that goes back to the application, including the identity provider's response</li> 
       <li>The application decodes the identity provider's response, and carries on accordingly.</li> 
       <li>(OAuth only) The response includes an access token which the application can use to gain direct access to the identity provider's services on the user's behalf.</li> 
      </ol> 
      <p>The crucial difference is that in the OpenID <i>authentication</i> use case, the response from the identity provider is an assertion of identity; while in the OAuth <i>authorization</i> use case, the identity provider is also an API provider, and the response from the identity provider is an access token that may grant the application ongoing access to some of the identity provider's APIs, on the user's behalf. The access token acts as a kind of "valet key" that the application can include with its requests to the identity provider, which prove that it has permission from the user to access those APIs.</p> 
      <p>Because the identity provider typically (but not always) authenticates the user as part of the process of granting an OAuth access token, it's tempting to view a successful OAuth access token request as an authentication method itself. However, because OAuth was not designed with this use case in mind, making this assumption can lead to major security flaws.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p> 
      <p><a href="/wiki/File:OpenIDvs.Pseudo-AuthenticationusingOAuth.svg" class="image" title="OpenID vs. pseudo-authentication using OAuth"><img alt="OpenID vs. pseudo-authentication using OAuth" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/OpenIDvs.Pseudo-AuthenticationusingOAuth.svg/512px-OpenIDvs.Pseudo-AuthenticationusingOAuth.svg.png" width="512" height="447" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/OpenIDvs.Pseudo-AuthenticationusingOAuth.svg/768px-OpenIDvs.Pseudo-AuthenticationusingOAuth.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/OpenIDvs.Pseudo-AuthenticationusingOAuth.svg/1024px-OpenIDvs.Pseudo-AuthenticationusingOAuth.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="447"></a></p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="OAuth_and_XACML">OAuth and XACML</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=OAuth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: OAuth and XACML">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p><a href="/wiki/XACML" title="XACML">XACML</a> is a policy-based, <a href="/wiki/Attribute-based_access_control" title="Attribute-based access control">attribute-based access control</a> authorization framework. It provides:</p> 
      <ul> 
       <li>An <a href="/wiki/XACML#Architecture" title="XACML">access control architecture</a>.</li> 
       <li>A policy language with which to express a wide range of access control policies including policies that can use consents handled / defined via OAuth.</li> 
       <li>A request / response scheme to send and receive authorization requests.</li> 
      </ul> 
      <p><a href="/wiki/XACML" title="XACML">XACML</a> and OAuth can be combined together to deliver a more comprehensive approach to authorization. OAuth does not provide a policy language with which to define access control policies. <a href="/wiki/XACML" title="XACML">XACML</a> can be used for its policy language.</p> 
      <p>Where OAuth focuses on delegated access (I, the user, grant Twitter access to my Facebook wall), and identity-centric authorization, <a href="/wiki/XACML" title="XACML">XACML</a> takes an attribute-based approach which can consider attributes of the user, the action, the resource, and the context (who, what, where, when, how). With <a href="/wiki/XACML" title="XACML">XACML</a> it is possible to define policies such as</p> 
      <ul> 
       <li>Managers can view documents in their department</li> 
       <li>Managers can edit documents they own in draft mode</li> 
      </ul> 
      <p><a href="/wiki/XACML" title="XACML">XACML</a> provides more fine-grained access control than OAuth does. OAuth is limited in granularity to the coarse functionality (the scopes) exposed by the target service. As a result, it often makes sense to combine OAuth and <a href="/wiki/XACML" title="XACML">XACML</a> together where OAuth will provide the delegated access use case and consent management and <a href="/wiki/XACML" title="XACML">XACML</a> will provide the authorization policies that work on the applications, processes, and data.</p> 
      <p>Lastly, <a href="/wiki/XACML" title="XACML">XACML</a> can work transparently across multiple stacks (APIs, web SSO, ESBs, home-grown apps, databases...). OAuth focuses exclusively on HTTP-based apps.</p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversy">Controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=OAuth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>In July 2012, Eran Hammer resigned his role of lead author for the OAuth 2.0 project, withdrew from the IETF working group, and removed his name from the specification. Hammer pointed to a conflict between the web and enterprise cultures, citing the IETF as a community that is "all about enterprise use cases", that is "not capable of simple." What is now offered is a blueprint for an authorization protocol, he says, and "that is the enterprise way", providing a "whole new frontier to sell consulting services and integration solutions."<sup id="cite_ref-oauthtwoandtheroadtohell_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oauthtwoandtheroadtohell-27">[27]</a></sup></p> 
      <p>In comparing OAuth 2.0 with 1.0, Hammer points out that it has become "more complex, less interoperable, less useful, more incomplete, and most importantly, less secure." He explains how architectural changes for 2.0 unbound tokens from clients, removed all signatures and cryptography at a protocol level and added expiring tokens (because tokens couldn't be revoked) while complicating the processing of authorization. Numerous items were left unspecified or unlimited in the specification because "as has been the nature of this working group, no issue is too small to get stuck on or leave open for each implementation to decide."<sup id="cite_ref-oauthtwoandtheroadtohell_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oauthtwoandtheroadtohell-27">[27]</a></sup></p> 
      <p>Hammer later gave a presentation at &amp;Yet elaborating on his views.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p> 
      <p><a href="/wiki/David_Recordon" title="David Recordon">David Recordon</a> later also removed his name from the specifications for unspecified reasons. <a href="/wiki/Dick_Hardt" title="Dick Hardt">Dick Hardt</a> took over the editor role, and the framework was published in October 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-Dick_Hardt_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dick_Hardt-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
