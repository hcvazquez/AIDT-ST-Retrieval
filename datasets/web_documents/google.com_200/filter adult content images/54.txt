          <p>A cigar is defined as a roll of tobacco wrapped in leaf tobacco or in a substance that contains tobacco.<sup>1,2</sup></p> 
          <p>Cigars differ from cigarettes in that cigarettes are a roll of tobacco wrapped in paper or in a substance that does not contain tobacco.</p> 
          <p>The three major types of cigars sold in the United States are large cigars, cigarillos, and little cigars.<sup>1,2</sup></p> 
          <p>The use of flavorings in some cigar brands and the fact that they are commonly sold as a single stick has raised concerns that these products may be especially appealing to youth.<sup>3,4,5,6</sup></p> 
          <p>In 2014, among middle and high school students who used cigars in the past 30 days, 63.5% reported using a flavored cigar during that time.<sup>6</sup></p> 
          <p>Little cigars are the same size and shape as cigarettes, often include a filter, and are packaged in a similar way, but they are taxed differently than cigarettes. Rather than reduce consumption, cost-conscious smokers might switch from cigarettes to less costly little cigars.<sup>2,5,7</sup></p> 
          <p>Historically, cigar smoking in the United States has been a behavior of older men, but the industry’s increased marketing of these products to targeted groups in the 1990s increased the prevalence of use among adolescents.<sup>3</sup></p> 
          <p>Cigar use is higher among youth who use other tobacco products or other drugs (e.g., alcohol, marijuana, and inhalants) than among youth who do not use these products.<sup>3</sup></p> 
          <br> 
          <div class="module-typeC"> 
           <div class="center">
            <br> 
            <h4><span class="emphasis">Cigars contain the same toxic and carcinogenic compounds found in cigarettes and are not a safe alternative to cigarettes.<sup>1,4</sup></span></h4> 
           </div> 
          </div> 
          <br>

          <h3 class="stu_section" id="effects">Health Effects</h3> 
          <div class="row span19"> 
           <div class="span9">
            <br> 
            <ul> 
             <li>Regular cigar smoking is associated with an increased risk for cancers of the lung, esophagus, larynx (voice box), and oral cavity (lip, tongue, mouth, throat).<sup>1,2</sup></li> 
             <li>Cigar smoking is linked to gum disease and tooth loss.<sup>2</sup></li> 
             <li>Heavy cigar smokers and those who inhale deeply may be at increased risk for developing coronary heart disease.<sup>1,2</sup></li> 
             <li>Heavy cigar smoking increases the risk for lung diseases, such as emphysema and chronic bronchitis.<sup>1,2</sup></li> 
            </ul> 
           </div> 
           <div class="span9"> 
            <p class="center"><img alt="Image of various forms of cigars" src="/tobacco/data_statistics/fact_sheets/tobacco_industry/cigars/images/cigars2.jpg"></p> 
           </div> 
          </div> 
          <br class="clearing"> 
          <a class="onThisPageAnchor" id="current" name="current" title="Current Cigar Use">
           <!-- --></a> 
          <h3 class="stu_section" id="current">Current Cigar Use</h3> 
          <h4 class="stu">Adults*</h4> 
          <p><span class="emphasis">Percentage of U.S. adults who were current cigar smokers<sup>†</sup> in 2013:</span><sup>9</sup></p> 
          <ul> 
           <li class="arrow1"><span class="highlight">5.0%</span>&nbsp;of&nbsp;<span class="highlight"> all adults </span><br> </li> 
          </ul> 
          <ul> 
           <li class="arrow1"><span class="highlight">8.2%</span>&nbsp;of&nbsp;<span class="highlight"> adult males</span></li> 
           <li class="arrow1"><span class="highlight">2.0%</span>&nbsp;of&nbsp;<span class="highlight"> adult females</span><br> </li> 
          </ul> 
          <ul> 
           <li class="arrow1"><span class="highlight">7.5%</span>&nbsp;of&nbsp;<span class="highlight"> African American adults</span></li> 
           <li class="arrow1"><span class="highlight">6.7%</span>&nbsp;of&nbsp;<span class="highlight"> American Indian/Alaska Native adults</span></li> 
           <li class="arrow1"><span class="highlight">2.1%</span>&nbsp;of&nbsp;<span class="highlight"> Asian American adults</span></li> 
           <li class="arrow1"><span class="highlight">4.0%</span>&nbsp;of&nbsp;<span class="highlight"> Hispanic adults</span></li> 
           <li class="arrow1"><span class="highlight">5.0%</span>&nbsp;of&nbsp;<span class="highlight"> White adults</span></li> 
          </ul> 
          <h4 class="stu">High School Students</h4> 
          <p><span class="emphasis">Percentage of U.S. high school students who were current smokers<sup>†</sup> in 2014:</span><sup>10</sup></p> 
          <ul> 
           <li class="arrow1"><span class="highlight">8.2%</span>&nbsp;of&nbsp;<span class="highlight">all students in grades 9–12</span></li> 
           <li class="arrow1"><span class="highlight">&nbsp;&nbsp;5.5%</span>&nbsp;of&nbsp;<span class="highlight">female students in grades 9–12</span></li> 
           <li class="arrow1"><span class="highlight">10.8%</span>&nbsp;of&nbsp;<span class="highlight">male students in grades 9–12</span><br> </li> 
          </ul> 
          <ul> 
           <li>Current (past 30 days) cigar use among high school males (10.8%) is almost double that of high school females (5.5%) and similar to cigarette use among high school males (10.6%).<sup>10</sup></li> 
          </ul> 
          <h4 class="stu">Middle School Students</h4> 
          <p><span class="emphasis">Percentage of U.S. middle school students who were current cigar smokers<sup>†</sup> in 2014:</span><sup>10</sup></p> 
          <ul> 
           <li class="arrow1"><span class="highlight">1.9%</span>&nbsp;of&nbsp;<span class="highlight">all U.S. students in grades 6–8</span></li> 
           <li class="arrow1"><span class="highlight">1.4%</span>&nbsp;of&nbsp;<span class="highlight">female students in grades 6–8</span></li> 
           <li class="arrow1"><span class="highlight">2.4%</span>&nbsp;of&nbsp;<span class="highlight">male students in grades 6–8</span><br> </li> 
          </ul> 
          <h4 class="stu"><span class="emphasis">Overall</span></h4> 
          <ul> 
           <li>In 2013, an estimated 12.4 million people in the United States aged 12 years or older (or 5.2%) were current cigar smokers.<sup>9</sup></li> 
          </ul> 
          <p class="descriptor">*Adults are defined as persons 18 years of age or older.<br> <sup>†</sup>Current cigar use is defined as smoking cigars on 1 or more of the 30 days before participation in a survey about this topic.</p> 
          <a class="onThisPageAnchor" id="marketing" name="marketing" title="Marketing Information">
           <!-- --></a> 
          <h3 class="stu_section" id="marketing">Marketing Information</h3> 
          <p><span class="emphasis">Marketing efforts promote cigars as symbols of a luxury and successful lifestyle. The following strategies can contribute to the increased acceptability of cigar smoking:</span><sup>1,3</sup></p> 
          <ul> 
           <li>Endorsements by celebrities</li> 
           <li>Development of cigar-friendly magazines (e.g., <em>Cigar Aficionado</em>)</li> 
           <li>Images of highly visible women smoking cigars</li> 
           <li>Product placement in movies</li> 
          </ul> 
          <br> 
          <p><span class="emphasis">In 2001, the Federal Trade Commission mandated that cigar packaging and advertisements must display one of the following five "SURGEON GENERAL WARNING" text-only labels on a rotating basis:</span><sup>11</sup></p> 
          <ul> 
           <li>Cigar Smoking Can Cause Cancers Of The Mouth And Throat, Even If You Do Not Inhale.</li> 
           <li>Cigar Smoking Can Cause Lung Cancer And Heart Disease.</li> 
           <li>Tobacco Use Increases The Risk Of Infertility, Stillbirth, And Low Birth Weight.</li> 
           <li>Cigars Are Not A Safe Alternative To Cigarettes.</li> 
           <li>Tobacco Smoke Increases The Risk Of Lung Cancer And Heart Disease, Even In Nonsmokers.</li> 
          </ul>
